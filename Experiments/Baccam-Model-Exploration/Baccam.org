* Setup
** Background

This notebook applies the AI-Aristotle-style grey-box identification framework
to the target cell-limited model of influenza A virus infection from Baccam et
al. (2006), /J. Virol./ 80(15):7590–7599.

The model tracks three populations — target epithelial cells (T), productively
infected cells (I), and free virus (V) — and was originally fit to nasal wash
viral titers from volunteers experimentally infected with H1N1 influenza
A/Hong Kong/123/77.

*** The full (true) model

#+NAME: eq:Baccam-true
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
&\frac{dT}{dt} = -\beta \, T \, V \\[4pt]
&\frac{dI}{dt} = \beta \, T \, V - \delta \, I \\[4pt]
&\frac{dV}{dt} = p \, I - c \, V
\end{align}
\end{subequations}

*** Published parameter values (geometric means from Baccam et al. Table 2)

#+NAME: table:Baccam-params
| Parameter | Description                     |           Value | Units                      |
|-----------+---------------------------------+-----------------+----------------------------|
| $T_0$     | Initial target cells            |      $4\times10^{8}$ | cells                      |
| $V_0$     | Initial viral inoculum          | $9.3\times10^{-2}$ | TCID_{50}/ml                   |
| $\beta$       | Infection rate constant          | $2.7\times10^{-5}$ | (TCID_{50}/ml)^{-1} · d^{-1}        |
| $p$       | Virus production rate per cell  | $1.2\times10^{-2}$ | TCID_{50}/ml · d^{-1} · cell^{-1} |
| $c$       | Viral clearance rate            |             3.0 | d^{-1}                       |
| $\delta$      | Infected cell death rate        |             4.0 | d^{-1}                       |

*** Grey-box formulation

We assume that the source and sink of /target cells/ (dT/dt) and the /virus
production/ term in dV/dt are unknown. The known physics is restricted to the
infected-cell equation. Two neural-network-approximated functions $g_T$ and
$g_V$ replace the missing terms:

#+NAME: eq:Baccam-greybox
\begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
&\frac{dT}{dt} = \mathrm{g}_T(t,\,\theta_g) \\[4pt]
&\frac{dI}{dt} = \beta \, T \, V - \delta \, I \\[4pt]
&\frac{dV}{dt} = \mathrm{g}_V(t,\,\theta_g) - c \, V
\end{align}
\end{subequations}

After training, symbolic regression is applied to $g_T$ and $g_V$ to recover
the analytical forms $g_T = -\beta\,T\,V$ and $g_V = p\,I$.

*** State-space transform

All three states span a range of ~0 to 10⁸. We apply a log₁₀ transform via
~LogTransform~ from ~Transforms.jl~: each state is mapped as

\[
  z_i = \log_{10}(y_i + \epsilon_{\text{floor}})
\]

where $\epsilon_{\text{floor}} = 10^{-6}$ prevents log(0). The ~transformed_rhs~
machinery in ~Transforms.jl~ automatically applies the chain-rule Jacobian so
that the architecture function can be written in *raw* (biological) coordinates.

Because the log-transformed states still span different ranges (roughly −6 to 8),
we further normalize by the per-state maximum of the transformed training data.
This is done via a ~ZScoreTransform~ composed after the log.  In practice we
chain them manually: log₁₀-transform the raw training targets, compute the
per-state max, then define a single ~LogTransform~ for the pipeline while
feeding the network min-max–scaled targets through a ~ZScoreTransform~ wrapper.

*Update:* Rather than hand-rolling the normalisation as before, we now use the
~LogTransform~ struct from ~Transforms.jl~ and pass it to ~train_fixed_hyper~
via the ~transform~ keyword. The architecture function (~BaccamArchitectureRaw~)
is written in raw biological coordinates; the framework's ~transformed_rhs~
handles the coordinate change in the ODE loss automatically.

*** Experimental design: lean vs wide sampling

In animal studies the total number of sacrificed animals is typically fixed by
ethical and budgetary constraints. We simulate this by holding the total
observation budget $N_{\text{total}} = n_{\text{timepoints}} \times
n_{\text{mice}}$ constant at 120 and exploring three configurations:

| Configuration | Timepoints | Mice / timepoint | Character        |
|---------------+------------+-------------------+------------------|
| A (lean)      |          5 |                24 | Few times, dense replicates |
| B (balanced)  |         10 |                12 | Moderate both    |
| C (wide)      |         20 |                 6 | Many times, lean replicates |

Each "mouse" is an independent noisy realisation of the ODE solution at that
timepoint. Noise is log-normal (multiplicative) to reflect biological
variability, and the per-timepoint mean across mice becomes the training signal.

The struct wraps the three ODE rate constants that appear in the known part of
the grey-box architecture — β (infection rate), δ (infected-cell death rate),
and c (viral clearance rate). Virus production rate /p/ is deliberately excluded
because it lives inside the unknown term $g_V$.

    c::T   # viral clearance rate
end

BaccamParams(nt::ComponentVector) = BaccamParams(nt.β, nt.δ, nt.c)

#+RESULTS:

** Generating the ground-truth dataset

We solve the full Baccam ODE with the published geometric-mean parameter
estimates from Table 2 of Baccam et al. (2006). The solution is evaluated on a
fine grid and then sub-sampled according to the three experimental
configurations defined above.

#+begin_src julia-vterm

# Full ODE right-hand side for DifferentialEquations.jl
function Baccam_model!(du, u, p, t)
    T, I, V = u
    du[1] = -p.β * T * V
    du[2] =  p.β * T * V - p.δ * I
    du[3] =  p.p * I     - p.c * V
end

# True parameters — geometric means from Baccam et al. (2006) Table 2
# All four params are packed here for data generation; the grey-box will only
# expose β, δ, c to the optimizer.
true_params = (β = 2.7f-5, δ = 4.0f0, p = 1.2f-2, c = 3.0f0)

# Initial conditions (T₀ from paper, I₀ = 0, V₀ geometric mean from Table 2)
u0 = Float32[4.0f8, 0.0f0, 9.3f-2]

# Timespan matches the 8-day observation window in the original study
t_span = (0.0f0, 8.0f0)

# Solve on a fine grid for later sub-sampling
N_fine = 2001
t_fine = range(t_span[1], t_span[2], length = N_fine) |> collect

prob = ODEProblem(
    Baccam_model!,
    u0,
    t_span,
    (β = true_params.β, δ = true_params.δ, p = true_params.p, c = true_params.c)
)

sol = solve(prob, Tsit5(); saveat = t_fine)
sol_array = Array(sol)  # 3 × N_fine
#+end_src

#+RESULTS:

** Creating the three sampling configurations

For each configuration we: (1) select the timepoint locations, (2) draw
~n_mice~ independent log-normal noise realisations per timepoint, and (3)
compute the per-timepoint mean across mice as the training signal. All three
datasets are saved as CSV files for reproducibility.

#+begin_src julia-vterm

# Reproducible RNG
rng = MersenneTwister(42)

# Log-normal multiplicative noise: X_obs = X_true * exp(σε − σ²/2),  ε ~ N(0,1)
noise_σ = 0.5f0

"""
    generate_config(rng, sol_array, t_fine, n_tp, n_mice, noise_σ)

Sub-sample the ODE solution at `n_tp` equally-spaced timepoints and generate
`n_mice` noisy replicates per timepoint. Returns a NamedTuple with raw data
and the mouse-averaged training matrix.
"""
function generate_config(rng, sol_array, t_fine, n_tp, n_mice, noise_σ)
    # Indices into the fine time grid (equally spaced, including endpoints)
    idx = round.(Int, range(1, length(t_fine), length = n_tp))
    t_obs = Float32.(t_fine[idx])               # n_tp
    Y_true = Float32.(sol_array[:, idx])         # 3 × n_tp

    # Draw n_mice independent noise realisations per timepoint
    ε = randn(rng, Float32, 3, n_tp, n_mice)
    Y_noisy = Y_true .* exp.(noise_σ .* ε .- 0.5f0 * noise_σ^2)
    Y_noisy = clamp.(Y_noisy, 0.0f0, Inf32)

    # Training signal: per-timepoint mean across mice
    Y_mean = dropdims(mean(Y_noisy; dims = 3); dims = 3)   # 3 × n_tp

    return (
        t_obs    = t_obs,
        Y_true   = Y_true,
        Y_noisy  = Y_noisy,   # 3 × n_tp × n_mice — kept for plotting
        Y_mean   = Y_mean,    # 3 × n_tp — this is the training target
        n_tp     = n_tp,
        n_mice   = n_mice,
    )
end

# Three configurations: total budget = 120
config_A = generate_config(rng, sol_array, t_fine, 5,  24, noise_σ)
config_B = generate_config(rng, sol_array, t_fine, 10, 12, noise_σ)
config_C = generate_config(rng, sol_array, t_fine, 20,  6, noise_σ)

configs = Dict("A_5x24" => config_A, "B_10x12" => config_B, "C_20x6" => config_C)

#+RESULTS:

** Setting up the log₁₀ state transform

We use ~LogTransform~ from ~Transforms.jl~ with base 10 and a small additive
floor of 1e-6 per state. This avoids log(0) for the I(0)=0 initial condition.
All three states are masked for logging since they all span many orders of
magnitude.

The ~transformed_rhs~ machinery in ~Transforms.jl~ will automatically apply the
chain-rule Jacobian dz/dt = (dy/dt) / ((y + shift) · ln(10)) when computing
the ODE residual, so the architecture function can stay in raw coordinates.

#+begin_src julia-vterm

# Floor value to prevent log(0); applied to all three states (T, I, V)
log_floor = 1f-6

# log₁₀ transform: z_i = log10(y_i + floor)
# All three states are transformed (mask = [true, true, true])
state_transform = LogTransform(
    10f0,                                          # base
    Float32[log_floor, log_floor, log_floor],      # per-state additive shift
    Bool[true, true, true],                        # mask: log all states
)
#+end_src

#+RESULTS:

* Building the grey-box architecture

The architecture function now operates in *raw biological coordinates*. The
~transformed_rhs~ wrapper from ~Transforms.jl~ handles the log₁₀ chain rule
when the ODE loss is evaluated in the transformed (log) space.

This is the key difference from the previous version: we no longer need a
separate "Norm" variant of the architecture. The same raw-coordinate function
works for any choice of state transform.

#+begin_src julia-vterm

function BaccamArchitectureRaw(
    y::AbstractMatrix{<:Real},   # 3×B  raw states (T, I, V)
    g::AbstractMatrix{<:Real},   # 2×B  learned terms (g_T, g_V) in raw coordinates
    p::BaccamParams,
)
    @assert size(y, 1) == 3 "y must have 3 states"
    @assert size(g, 1) == 2 "g must have 2 outputs"

    T = y[1, :]
    I = y[2, :]
    V = y[3, :]

    g_T = g[1, :]   # should learn → −β·T·V
    g_V = g[2, :]   # should learn → p·I

    # Known physics only in the I-equation; g_T and g_V are fully learned
    dT = g_T
    dI = p.β .* T .* V .- p.δ .* I
    dV = g_V .- p.c .* V

    return permutedims(hcat(dT, dI, dV))
end
#+end_src

#+RESULTS:

* Initialising the MLPs

The state network maps time → 3 states in transformed (log₁₀) space; the
g-network maps time → 2 unknown source terms. Output activations are chosen to
match the expected range of the transformed training targets.

Because the log₁₀-transformed training targets span roughly [−6, 8] (then
further scaled by the per-state max via ZScoreTransform inside the pipeline),
we keep the sigmoid output on the state MLP to bound predictions to [0, 1]
when combined with a ZScoreTransform, or use an unbounded output if the
pipeline's internal normalization handles it.

Note: The pipeline's ~build_contexts~ applies ~forward_state(transform, ·)~ to
the raw training data and IC, so the network sees log₁₀-transformed targets.
We use sigmoid to keep predictions in a reasonable range.

#+begin_src julia-vterm

# State MLP: t ↦ (T̃, Ĩ, Ṽ) in log₁₀-transformed coordinates
# Sigmoid output matches [0, 1] when combined with per-state normalization
build_state_mlp() = Lux.Chain(
    Lux.Dense(1, 8, tanh_fast),
    Lux.Dense(8, 6, tanh_fast),
    Lux.Dense(6, 3)
)

# Physics-residual MLP: t ↦ (g_T, g_V) — unbounded since source terms can be negative
build_g_mlp() = Lux.Chain(
    Lux.Dense(1, 6, tanh_fast),
    Lux.Dense(6, 4, tanh_fast),
    Lux.Dense(4, 2)
)
#+end_src

#+RESULTS:

* Callback for diagnostic logging

The callback records data MSE, ODE MSE, and g-network standard deviation at
regular intervals during training. The history vectors are stored in a
dictionary keyed by configuration label so that runs don't clobber each other.

#+begin_src julia-vterm

# Global store: config label → NamedTuple of history vectors
diag_histories = Dict{String, NamedTuple}()

"""
    make_baccam_callback(label; log_every=25, print_every=25_000)

Return a callback closure that logs loss diagnostics into `diag_histories[label]`.
Prints a status line every `print_every` iterations.
"""
function make_baccam_callback(label; log_every=25, print_every=25_000)
    # Fresh history vectors for this config
    hist = (
        iter     = Int[],
        data_mse = Float32[],
        ode_mse  = Float32[],
        g_std    = Float32[],
    )
    diag_histories[label] = hist

    function cb(state, l)
        if state.iter % log_every == 0
            if state.iter % print_every == 0
                println("  [$label] iter=$(state.iter)")
            end

            ps  = state.u
            ctx = state.p

            d_mse = loss_Data(ps, ctx)

            # ODE and g metrics are only meaningful in Stage 2
            if ctx isa PINNCtxStage2
                o_mse = loss_ODE(ps, ctx, BaccamArchitectureRaw, BaccamParams)
                gmodel = g_model(ps, ctx)
                g_out  = gmodel(ctx.t_dense)
                g_s    = std(vec(g_out))
            else
                o_mse = NaN32
                g_s   = NaN32
            end

            push!(hist.iter,     state.iter)
            push!(hist.data_mse, d_mse)
            push!(hist.ode_mse,  o_mse)
            push!(hist.g_std,    g_s)
        end
        return false
    end

    return cb
end

"""
    train_config(label, cfg, hp, u0, t_span, state_transform)

Prepare data and run the two-stage training for a single sampling configuration
using the LogTransform from Transforms.jl. The architecture function operates
in raw coordinates; the transform handles the chain-rule Jacobian automatically.
"""
function train_config(label, cfg, hp, u0, t_span, state_transform)
    println("\n" * "="^60)
    println("  [Thread $(Threads.threadid())] Training: $label")
    println("  Timepoints = $(cfg.n_tp),  Mice/tp = $(cfg.n_mice)")
    println("="^60)

    t_train = permutedims(cfg.t_obs)       # 1 × n_tp
    Y_train_raw = cfg.Y_mean               # 3 × n_tp (raw biological units)

    # Dense collocation grid for the ODE residual loss
    t_dense = permutedims(Float32.(range(
        minimum(t_train), maximum(t_train); length = 1000
    )))
    t_span_vec = Float32[minimum(t_train), maximum(t_train)]

    # Initial guesses for the known ODE parameters (deliberately offset)
    guess_params = BaccamParams(1.0f-4, 1.0f0, 1.0f0)

    # Parameter bounds (generous to test discovery)
    β_bounds = Float32[0f0, 1f-2]
    δ_bounds = Float32[0f0, 20f0]
    c_bounds = Float32[0f0, 20f0]

    # Pack the data tuple — note Y_train is raw; the pipeline's build_contexts
    # will apply forward_state(state_transform, ·) to produce log₁₀ targets.
    # y0_init_raw tells the pipeline to use the raw IC before transforming.
    data = (
        t_train        = t_train,
        Y_train        = Y_train_raw,
        Y_train_std    = ones(Float32, 3, 1),
        t_dense        = t_dense,
        t_span         = t_span_vec,
        y0_init        = u0,
        ODE_par_init   = (β = guess_params.β, δ = guess_params.δ, c = guess_params.c),
        ODE_par_bounds = (β = β_bounds, δ = δ_bounds, c = c_bounds),
    )

    # Per-config callback — writes to its own slot in diag_histories
    cb = make_baccam_callback(label; log_every = 25, print_every = 25_000)

    stats = @timed res = train_fixed_hyper(
        data,
        BaccamArchitectureRaw,
        BaccamParams,
        hp;
        transform            = state_transform,
        user_loss_functions  = Function[],
        maxiters_stage1      = 50_000,
        maxiters_stage2      = 120_000,
        Opt_alg_stage1       = OptimizationOptimisers.Adam(1f-4),
        Opt_alg_stage2       = OptimizationOptimisers.Adam(1f-4),
        # callback_function    = cb,
    )

    println("  [$label] Wall time: $(round(stats.time; digits=1)) s")
    println("  [$label] Data MSE:  $(res.metrics.data_mse)")
    println("  [$label] ODE MSE:   $(res.metrics.ode_mse)")
    println("  [$label] Recovered β = $(res.params.ODE_par.β)")
    println("  [$label] Recovered δ = $(res.params.ODE_par.δ)")
    println("  [$label] Recovered c = $(res.params.ODE_par.c)")

    return (
        res    = res,
        cfg    = cfg,
        data   = data,
        time_s = stats.time,
    )
end

* Symbolic regression on the recovered g-functions

We extract the state and g-network outputs on the dense training grid and use
~SymbolicRegression.jl~ to distil closed-form expressions. The state MLP
outputs are in log₁₀-transformed coordinates; we invert them back to raw units
so that the symbolic expressions are directly interpretable in biological terms.

#+begin_src julia-vterm

using SymbolicRegression

for key in config_keys
    col_idx = findfirst(==(key), config_keys)
    label = config_labels[col_idx]
    println("\n", "="^60)
    println("  Symbolic regression for $label")
    println("="^60)

    r   = results[key]
    res = r.res

    # State and g models on the dense grid
    smodel = state_model(res.params, res.ctx2)
    gmodel = g_model(res.params, res.ctx2)

    t_dense = res.ctx2.t_dense
    Ỹ = smodel(t_dense)     # 3 × B  (log₁₀-transformed states)
    G̃ = gmodel(t_dense)     # 2 × B  (learned g_T, g_V)

    # Invert to raw biological coordinates for interpretable SR features
    Y_raw = inverse_state(state_transform, Ỹ)

    # Feature matrix: raw states as rows (T, I, V in biological units)
    X = Float64.(Array(Y_raw))   # 3 × B

    # ---- Recover g_T (should find ∝ −T·V) ----
    println("  >> Searching for g_T ...")
    opts_gT = SymbolicRegression.Options(
        binary_operators = [+, -, *],
        unary_operators  = [],
        npopulations     = 30,
        maxsize          = 20,
    )
    hof_gT = SymbolicRegression.EquationSearch(
        X, Float64.(vec(G̃[1, :]));
        niterations    = 200,
        options        = opts_gT,
        progress       = false,
        variable_names = ["T", "I", "V"],
    )
    pareto_gT = SymbolicRegression.calculate_pareto_frontier(hof_gT)
    println("  g_T Pareto front ($(length(pareto_gT)) expressions):")
    for m in pareto_gT
        println("    complexity=$(m.complexity)  loss=$(round(m.loss; sigdigits=4))  $(m.tree)")
    end

    # ---- Recover g_V (should find ∝ I) ----
    println("  >> Searching for g_V ...")
    opts_gV = SymbolicRegression.Options(
        binary_operators = [+, -, *],
        unary_operators  = [],
        npopulations     = 30,
        maxsize          = 20,
    )
    hof_gV = SymbolicRegression.EquationSearch(
        X, Float64.(vec(G̃[2, :]));
        niterations    = 200,
        options        = opts_gV,
        progress       = false,
        variable_names = ["T", "I", "V"],
    )
    pareto_gV = SymbolicRegression.calculate_pareto_frontier(hof_gV)
    println("  g_V Pareto front ($(length(pareto_gV)) expressions):")
    for m in pareto_gV
        println("    complexity=$(m.complexity)  loss=$(round(m.loss; sigdigits=4))  $(m.tree)")
    end

* Summary and interpretation

The three configurations test how temporal resolution versus replicate depth
trade off when the total observation budget is fixed:

- *Config A (5 × 24)*: Very few timepoints but many mice. The per-timepoint
  mean is accurate (low noise), but the temporal dynamics are poorly resolved.
  This is the hardest setting for the ODE-residual loss, which relies on smooth
  time derivatives estimated by finite differences.

- *Config B (10 × 12)*: A balanced design. Moderate noise reduction via
  averaging, with enough temporal coverage for the finite-difference ODE loss to
  work reasonably.

- *Config C (20 × 6)*: Dense temporal coverage with only six mice per point.
  The training signal is noisier, but the optimiser has many collocation-like
  anchor points and can resolve the dynamics well.

The key metric is whether symbolic regression can recover the ground-truth
functional forms $g_T = -\beta\,T\,V$ and $g_V = p\,I$ from the learned
g-network. We expect Config C (wide) to favour recovery because PINNs and
finite-difference ODE losses benefit from dense temporal sampling, while Config
A (lean) may struggle despite having cleaner per-point estimates.

*Transform note:* By switching from the hand-rolled ~LogMinMaxTransform~ to the
~LogTransform~ from ~Transforms.jl~, we gain two advantages: (1) the chain-rule
Jacobian for the ODE residual is computed automatically and correctly by
~transformed_rhs~, and (2) the same architecture function (~BaccamArchitectureRaw~)
works unchanged if we later swap to a different transform (e.g. ~ZScoreTransform~
or ~IdentityTransform~).
